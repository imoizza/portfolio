TrackZone — platform overview

01 — Context

The question the whole platform had to answer

How might we help cities, industries, and waste collection companies make data-driven decisions through real-time waste insights, efficient routing, and automation?

TrackZone runs on physical sensors bolted to real bins in the field, which meant every design decision had a hardware constraint sitting underneath it — camera angles, battery life, offline connectivity. I worked across four roles on this one: product strategist, UX researcher, UI designer, and project manager running the agile process end to end.

02 — Research

Research done in the field, not a lab

Because this was enterprise hardware, most insight came directly from client meetings — municipal representatives, drivers, and internal hardware engineers — rather than a formal usability panel.

  • Overflow misdetection traced back to poor fill-level calibration and incorrect camera sensor angles — a hardware problem the interface needed to surface and explain, not hide.
  • Poor route visibility — admins had no live feedback on coverage, and drivers navigated with no real guidance.
  • Slow reconfiguration — onboarding a new area meant manual bin setup and downlink updates, which didn't scale.

03 — Design

Ten modules, one coherent system

The platform split into modular pieces that could ship independently while staying visually and functionally consistent: Bins Monitoring (offline/online/full/overflowing states, tamper and fire alerts), Track & Trace (GPS-based fleet visibility with geofencing), Vision AI (image-based fill and contamination detection across waste types), and Route Optimization — both a manual mode for dispatchers and a dynamic mode that auto-generates routes from live fill data.

Beyond that: location-based clustering with drag-drop reassignment, a downlink manager for batch device configuration, a sustainability KPI dashboard tracking diversion rates, and a three-tier subscription system built on Mollie for payments. A companion mobile app split cleanly by role — admins get the live map and provisioning tools, drivers get guided navigation to depots and dump sites.

The hardest part wasn't any single screen — it was translating sensor thresholds, camera positioning, and downlink timing into interfaces that felt simple despite the complexity underneath.

04 — Outcome

A hardware-dominated industry, made SaaS-shaped

TrackZone reduced overflow incidents, cut route time through dynamic optimization, and used contamination detection to lift recycling quality — while introducing a genuinely scalable SaaS layer into an industry that had previously been hardware-only.

Running this as PM as well as designer meant every interface decision was already pressure-tested against sprint reality — what firmware could actually deliver, what backend could realistically expose, and what a municipal client would actually pay for.
