Redesigning the commute for Lahore's night-shift workforce

A scheduled-ride app for employees navigating Lahore's unreliable public transport — built around flexible scheduling, cashless payments, and an SOS feature for the commutes that happen after dark.

Role

UI/UX Designer

Timeline

2 weeks

Team

Small design team

Tools

Figma, FigJam

My Rider — target audience & positioning

01 — Context

Getting to work is not supposed to be the hard part

My Rider partners with companies in Lahore to run scheduled cabs, buses, and vans for employees, standing in for a public transport system that's unreliable enough to make people late — or unsafe enough that late shifts feel risky. This case study covers the employee-facing side of the app.

Two weeks, one clear brief: make the daily commute reliable, flexible, and safe — especially after dark.

02 — Research

Ayesha's day

User interviews and journey mapping surfaced the same handful of needs repeatedly: flexible ride scheduling, secure in-app payments, and — raised unprompted by almost everyone doing late shifts — a way to signal for help quickly if a ride felt unsafe.

One persona-driven story from research stuck with the whole project: Ayesha finishes a night shift, adjusts her pickup time from the calendar screen, checks her wallet balance before the ride — and one night, feeling unsafe, taps SOS. Her live location goes straight to her emergency contact. That single moment shaped how much weight the safety feature carried in the final design.

03 — Design

Designing around the SOS moment, not around it

Rather than treating safety as one feature among many, I designed the whole flow so an SOS trigger was never more than a tap away, regardless of what screen someone was on — home location, shift scheduling, and the in-app wallet all sit around that same principle of fast, low-friction access when it matters.

She quickly taps the SOS button — her live location is shared instantly with her emergency contact. She feels more secure knowing help is a tap away.

The visual system stayed intentionally quiet — Inter throughout, a restrained component set — so the interface never competed with the one job that mattered most: getting someone home.

04 — Outcome & reflection

What two weeks actually taught me

Usability testing validated the core flows — scheduling, wallet, and SOS — as fast and low-friction as intended. More broadly, this project taught me to hold user empathy and business goals in the same hand: prioritizing the SOS feature wasn't just the emotionally obvious choice, it was also the one that made the whole product trustworthy enough to use daily.

Great design here wasn't about adding more — it was about making a necessary, sometimes stressful part of someone's day feel effortless.
