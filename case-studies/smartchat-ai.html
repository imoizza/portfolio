Mobile · Accessibility · 001

A floating chat that keeps up when you're multitasking

Redesigning an AI chat app around speech, not screens — so voice input, text-to-speech, and a floating window let people keep talking to it while doing something else entirely.

Role

UI/UX Designer

Timeline

4 weeks

Team

Solo project

Tools

Figma, Notion

SmartChat — marketing landing page

01 — Context

Voice features that nobody could find

SmartChat's speech-to-text and text-to-speech features worked, technically — but the interface around them was cluttered enough that people who actually needed hands-free communication (multitaskers, people on calls, anyone who just wanted to talk instead of type) gave up on discovering them.

The brief was narrow on purpose: make voice-first communication actually usable while doing something else, in four weeks, solo.

02 — Research

Watching how people already talk to AI

Rather than starting from a blank page, I ran contextual inquiry sessions watching people use ChatGPT's text-to-speech and Copilot's speech-to-text — specifically how they moved between apps while doing it.

  • Both tools helped, but neither supported real multitasking — switching apps broke the flow of conversation entirely.
  • People kept describing a want for something that could "float" above whatever they were already doing, rather than take over the screen.
  • This reframed accessibility for the project: not a feature to bolt on, but the core interaction model.

03 — Design

A window that follows you, not the other way around

The centerpiece is a floating icon anchored bottom-right, always reachable, opening a resizable chat window that adapts by roughly 20% depending on conversation length instead of staying a fixed size. Everything important — starting a chat, replaying a message, adjusting voice speed — is reachable in 2–3 clicks.

I applied Shneiderman's design principles throughout: consistency, user control, and shortcuts for people who'd use this daily rather than once. A small but telling detail — a speaker icon under every message — made text-to-speech discoverable without any onboarding tooltip explaining it.

Ten features shipped in the end: floating icon and chat, speech-to-text, text-to-speech, voice adjustments, font adjustment, theme customization, history, trendy prompts, and personalized recommendations.

Brand identity followed the same logic — a speech bubble mark with a rounded, three-dot strip standing in for both "message in progress" and "AI thinking," in a bright trust-signaling blue, set in SF Pro Display.

04 — Outcome

What A/B testing actually changed

Testing across layout variants showed two clear wins: navigation felt smoother once history moved into the bottom navigation bar, and speech recognition accuracy — or at least people's confidence in it — improved with clearer visual feedback during input.

Light mode ended up as the default after testing showed better readability for most people, and I added a notification bell so users could step away from an active chat without losing track of a reply — a small fix for the exact multitasking problem the whole project started from.
